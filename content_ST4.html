Nepal: A Climate-Vulnerable Nation

Nepal is among the world's most climate-vulnerable countries. Ranked consistently in the top ten most at-risk nations by the Global Climate Risk Index, Nepal faces threats from glacial retreat, erratic monsoons, floods, landslides, droughts, and increasingly severe weather events — despite contributing less than 0.1% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

This fundamental injustice — extreme vulnerability combined with minimal responsibility — makes climate policy both urgent and deeply political for Nepal. The country must simultaneously adapt to changes it did not cause, build resilience in some of the world's most challenging terrain, and advocate loudly in international forums for the rights of climate-vulnerable nations.

Nepal's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC)

Under the Paris Agreement, every country submits a Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) — a national climate action plan outlining commitments to reduce emissions and adapt to climate change. Nepal submitted its updated NDC in 2020, with ambitious but realistic targets rooted in the country's circumstances.

Key Commitments in Nepal's NDC

  • Net-zero emissions by 2045 — one of the most ambitious targets among developing nations, conditional on receiving adequate international climate finance.
  • 90% electricity from renewables by 2030 — largely achievable given Nepal's hydropower base.
  • 25% electric vehicles by 2025 and 90% by 2030 — an ambitious EV transition target for public and private transport.
  • Forest cover maintained at 45% of national territory — protecting Nepal's forests as critical carbon sinks and biodiversity reserves.
  • Reducing cooking-related emissions — supporting the transition from firewood and kerosene to clean cooking solutions for Nepal's rural households.

The Conditionality Problem

Most of Nepal's NDC commitments are "conditional" — meaning they can only be achieved if Nepal receives adequate international climate finance. The country has estimated it needs over USD 3.4 billion to implement its NDC fully. As of 2024, international climate finance flows to Nepal remain far below this figure — a gap that highlights the inadequacy of global climate finance mechanisms for small, vulnerable nations.

Loss & Damage: Nepal's Climate Justice Claim

Beyond mitigation and adaptation, Nepal has been a vocal advocate for "loss and damage" — the concept that wealthy, high-emitting countries owe compensation to vulnerable nations for the irreversible harm climate change has already caused. For Nepal, loss and damage is not abstract: it includes lives lost to floods and landslides, glacial lake outburst floods destroying downstream villages, reduced crop yields from erratic monsoons, and the forced migration of rural communities whose livelihoods climate change has made unviable.

The Historic COP27 Loss & Damage Fund

At COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh in 2022, countries agreed to establish a dedicated Loss and Damage fund — a historic breakthrough after decades of advocacy by climate- vulnerable nations including Nepal. Nepal's negotiators and civil society representatives played an important role in building the coalition that achieved this breakthrough. However, the fund's scope, governance, and financing remain contested.

Nepal's position: As a member of the Climate Vulnerable Forum (CVF) and the V20 group of financially vulnerable countries, Nepal consistently advocates for higher climate ambition from major emitters, stronger loss and damage mechanisms, and accessible, grant-based climate finance for vulnerable nations.

Adaptation: Building Resilience in Nepal

While mitigation (reducing emissions) is crucial globally, for Nepal the more immediate priority is adaptation — building the resilience of communities, infrastructure, and ecosystems to the climate changes that are already underway and will continue regardless of global emissions trajectories.

Early Warning Systems

Nepal's Department of Hydrology and Meteorology, supported by international partners, has developed early warning systems for floods and GLOFs in high-risk river basins. These systems have saved lives by giving communities hours of advance warning before flash floods — though coverage remains incomplete and technology-dependent.

Climate-Smart Agriculture

As monsoon patterns shift and temperatures rise, Nepal's farmers are experimenting with drought-resistant crop varieties, adjusted planting calendars, and water-harvesting techniques. The government's Climate Smart Village programme, supported by CGIAR and international NGOs, is helping communities adapt their farming systems to new climate realities.

Ecosystem-Based Adaptation

Nepal's forests are not only carbon sinks — they also regulate water flow, prevent erosion, and protect communities from floods and landslides. Maintaining and restoring forest cover in watershed areas is therefore one of Nepal's most cost-effective adaptation strategies. Community forestry — in which local communities manage forests sustainably — has been one of Nepal's great development success stories since the 1990s.

Youth Voice in Nepal's Climate Politics

Nepal's youth climate movement is growing rapidly. Young Nepalis are increasingly vocal — in streets, on social media, in schools and universities, and in international climate negotiations — about the injustice of facing catastrophic climate impacts for emissions they did not cause.

Youth for Climate Nepal

Youth for Climate Nepal coordinates young climate activists across the country, organises demonstrations, trains youth negotiators for international climate conferences, and runs awareness campaigns on campuses and in communities. The organisation has sent youth delegates to COP meetings, ensuring Nepali youth voices are heard in the rooms where global climate decisions are made.

The Fridays for Future Movement

Nepal's Fridays for Future chapter, inspired by the global movement started by Greta Thunberg, has held regular climate strikes in Kathmandu, Pokhara, and other cities. These strikes — often led by students and young professionals — keep climate change in the public conversation and pressure politicians to take stronger action.

What Students Can Do Right Now

Climate policy is shaped by pressure from citizens — including students. Your voice, your vote, and your activism matter. Here is how to engage:

  • Learn about Nepal's NDC and share its commitments with peers — public awareness creates political accountability.
  • Join Youth for Climate Nepal or start a climate action group at your university.
  • Participate in Fridays for Future strikes — visible, peaceful demonstrations signal to politicians that youth care about climate.
  • Engage with local government: Attend ward-level meetings and advocate for climate-smart urban planning in your municipality.
  • Amplify Nepal's climate justice case on social media using #LossAndDamage, #ClimateJustice, and #SDG13.
  • Study climate policy: Economics, law, political science, and environmental science students have valuable skills Nepal's climate negotiating teams need.
  • Vote: When you are eligible, support candidates with strong, credible climate commitments.
Remember: Nepal's position as a climate-vulnerable nation gives its citizens a powerful moral argument on the world stage. Use that voice. Share Nepal's story. The planet is listening.
