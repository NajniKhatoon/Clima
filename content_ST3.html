Green Consumption in Nepal

Consumption, Waste & Climate in Nepal

Nepal's rapid urbanisation has transformed consumption patterns. In Kathmandu and growing cities like Pokhara and Biratnagar, the rise of packaged goods, fast fashion, and single-use plastics has created a waste crisis that the country's infrastructure is ill-equipped to handle. Open burning of waste — a common practice — releases black carbon and greenhouse gases that contribute both to local air pollution and global climate change.

Yet Nepal also has deeply rooted traditions of sustainable consumption: community composting, seasonal eating, hand-me-down clothing, and the repair economy that thrives in local markets. Strengthening these traditions while adapting to modern life is at the heart of green consumption in Nepal.

Nepal's Urban Waste Crisis

Kathmandu generates over 600 tonnes of solid waste every day, yet collection and disposal systems remain severely inadequate. Waste is often dumped in rivers — including the sacred Bagmati — in open fields, or burned in the open air. The Sisdol landfill, the valley's main dumping ground, has been operating beyond capacity for years and has been a source of conflict with surrounding communities.

Open Burning: A Climate and Health Disaster

When waste is burned in open piles — as it routinely is across Nepal — it releases a toxic cocktail of gases including carbon dioxide, methane, dioxins, and black carbon. Black carbon is a particularly potent short-lived climate pollutant, with a warming effect per unit mass far greater than CO₂. It also settles on Himalayan glaciers, darkening them and accelerating melting.

Climate fact: Methane from decomposing organic waste in Nepal's poorly managed landfills and open dumps is a significant and underreported source of the country's greenhouse gas emissions.

Nepal's Plastic Pollution Problem

Single-use plastics — bags, bottles, straws, and food packaging — have proliferated rapidly across Nepal over the past two decades. They are now found in rivers, rice paddies, mountain trails, and glacial moraines. Nepal's beloved trekking routes in Annapurna and Everest regions have become globally notorious for plastic waste, despite the efforts of many trekking operators and conservation groups.

Nepal's Plastic Ban

Nepal has made several attempts to ban single-use plastics, including in national parks and protected areas. The government announced a ban on plastic bags thinner than 40 microns as far back as 2011, yet enforcement has been uneven. More recently, municipalities including Kathmandu have moved to restrict single-use plastics in public spaces with greater commitment to enforcement.

Mountains of Plastic: The Everest Problem

Sagarmatha (Everest) National Park receives tens of thousands of trekkers and climbers annually, many of whom leave behind plastic waste, oxygen canisters, and even tent equipment. Regular clean-up expeditions — led by organisations including the Sagarmatha Pollution Control Committee — have removed tonnes of waste from high altitudes, but the challenge requires systemic solutions including waste management infrastructure, deposit return schemes, and stronger permit conditions.

Food, Agriculture & Climate

Nepal's agriculture sector — employing around a quarter of the workforce — is both a victim and a contributor to climate change. Changing monsoon patterns, erratic rainfall, and rising temperatures are already reducing crop yields across the Terai plains and hill terraces that have sustained Nepali communities for centuries.

Traditional Sustainable Agriculture

Nepal's traditional hill farming practices — including terrace agriculture, agroforestry, and seed saving — are inherently low-carbon and biodiversity-rich. These practices are increasingly threatened by the allure of chemical-intensive commercial farming, which offers short-term yield gains at the cost of soil health, water quality, and long-term resilience.

Food Waste

Significant food is wasted along Nepal's supply chain due to inadequate storage, poor roads, and lack of refrigeration. Reducing post-harvest losses — through improved storage, better roads, and cold chain infrastructure — would not only reduce methane emissions from decomposing food but improve food security and farmer incomes simultaneously.

Community Solutions in Nepal

Clean Up Nepal: Founded by young Nepali activists, Clean Up Nepal has mobilised over 100,000 volunteers across the country for street, river, and trail clean-ups — proving that youth-led action can transform communities.

Kathmandu Waste Management: Community Composting

Several Kathmandu wards have successfully piloted community composting programmes, turning organic kitchen waste — which makes up 70% of the city's waste stream — into compost for urban gardens and farms. Ward 19 in Lalitpur has become a model for decentralised waste management, with door-to-door collection, waste segregation at source, and a community composting centre serving hundreds of households.

The Doko Recyclers Model

Doko Recyclers, a Kathmandu-based social enterprise, collects recyclable materials directly from households and offices, processes them, and sells them to recycling facilities. By professionalising the informal waste-picker economy, Doko has improved livelihoods while increasing recycling rates in areas it serves.

Bagmati Civilisation Project

The Bagmati River Civilisation Project represents Nepal's most ambitious urban environmental restoration effort. By relocating informal settlements from the flood plain, cleaning the river, and creating riverside parks and cycling paths, the project is transforming Kathmandu's relationship with its most sacred river.

What Students Can Do

As a student in Nepal, your consumption choices, your voice, and your community connections are all powerful tools for change.

  • Segregate waste at source: Separate organic, recyclable, and non-recyclable waste — the single most impactful household action for improving waste management.
  • Carry a reusable bag and water bottle: Refuse single-use plastic at every opportunity.
  • Compost organic kitchen waste: Even a small composting setup on a rooftop or balcony diverts waste from landfill and produces valuable fertiliser.
  • Buy second-hand and repair before replacing: Nepal's traditional repair economy is both sustainable and economically empowering.
  • Volunteer with Clean Up Nepal: Monthly events in cities and on popular trekking routes welcome student volunteers.
  • Advocate on campus: Lobby for single-use plastic bans, better recycling facilities, and food waste composting at your university.
  • Choose local and seasonal food: Eating locally grown, seasonal produce reduces the carbon footprint of your diet and supports Nepali farmers.
At your university: Advocate for your institution to adopt a comprehensive sustainability policy including waste reduction targets, a plastic-free campus commitment, and a community garden or composting programme.

Success Stories from Nepal

Pokhara's Plastic-Free Lake Campaign

Pokhara's lakeside area, centred on Phewa Lake, has seen sustained community action to reduce plastic pollution. Local businesses, trekking operators, and the municipal government have collaborated to restrict single-use plastics near the lake, install waste bins, and run regular clean-up campaigns. The lake's water quality has measurably improved in sections where these efforts have been most concentrated.

Sagarmatha Pollution Control Committee

Operating since 1991, the SPCC has collected over 100 tonnes of waste from Everest base camp and surrounding trails. The organisation trains porters in waste management, operates waste deposits at key checkpoints, and advocates for stronger regulations on expedition waste. Its work has become a model for high-altitude waste management worldwide.

Youth Innovation Lab Nepal

Based in Kathmandu, Youth Innovation Lab Nepal supports young people to design and implement environmental solutions including waste reduction campaigns, recycling awareness programmes, and clean technology innovations. Their incubation programme has supported dozens of youth-led sustainability enterprises across Nepal.
