Nepal's Transport & Climate Challenge

Kathmandu Valley is one of South Asia's most polluted urban areas. Sandwiched between mountains that trap emissions, the valley's air quality regularly exceeds World Health Organization safe limits by ten times or more. The primary culprit is vehicular emissions — from an aging, diesel-heavy fleet of buses, trucks, and private cars that has grown dramatically as Nepal's economy has expanded.

Transport accounts for a significant share of Nepal's total greenhouse gas emissions, and the sector's emissions are rising rapidly. Transitioning to sustainable, low-carbon transport is therefore not just a climate imperative — it is a public health emergency for Nepal's urban residents.

The Air Pollution Crisis in Kathmandu

Nepal's aging diesel vehicle fleet is a major source of urban air pollution.

Kathmandu's topography makes it a natural pollution trap. Surrounded by hills on three sides, the valley experiences frequent temperature inversions — atmospheric conditions that trap cold, polluted air at ground level beneath warmer air above. This means that emissions from vehicles, brick kilns, and construction sites accumulate with nowhere to go.

The health consequences are severe. Respiratory disease is among the leading causes of hospital admissions in Kathmandu. Children are disproportionately affected, with stunted lung development in areas near busy roads. The economic cost of health impacts from Kathmandu's air pollution runs to billions of rupees annually.

Climate Connection The same fossil fuels driving Nepal's air pollution crisis are also driving the climate change that is melting Himalayan glaciers, disrupting monsoons, and threatening the water security of millions of Nepalis. Solving transport emissions tackles both problems simultaneously.

Safa Tempo: Nepal's Electric Transport Pioneer

Kathmandu has a surprising and inspiring transport success story: the Safa Tempo. These three-wheeled electric vehicles — the name means "clean vehicle" in Nepali — have been operating in Kathmandu since the early 1990s. At their peak, over 600 Safa Tempos served routes across the valley, replacing polluting diesel-powered three-wheelers.

Rise, Decline, and Revival

Sadly, the number of Safa Tempos declined significantly through the 2000s and 2010s as policy support wavered and cheap diesel vehicles flooded the market. However, a renewed government push for electric vehicles — driven partly by Nepal's desire to use its own hydropower rather than import fossil fuels — is reviving interest in electric public transport.

Electric Buses: The Next Step

The government of Nepal has announced ambitious plans to electrify public bus routes in Kathmandu and other major cities. Chinese-manufactured electric buses are already operating on several routes, and Nepal's own hydropower gives the country an extraordinary advantage: electric vehicles here run on genuinely clean, renewable electricity — unlike in countries where the grid is still coal-dominated.

Cycling & Walking: Simple, Powerful Solutions

For short urban journeys — which make up the majority of trips in most cities — walking and cycling produce zero emissions and require no fuel imports. Yet Kathmandu's streets are notoriously hostile to cyclists and pedestrians: narrow, potholed roads shared with aggressive traffic, and almost no protected cycling infrastructure.

The Bagmati Cycling Corridor

The Bagmati River Civilisation Project has created a riverside pathway that serves as one of Kathmandu's few genuinely safe cycling and walking routes. On weekends, thousands of residents use this green corridor for exercise, commuting, and leisure — demonstrating the latent demand for safe active travel infrastructure.

Cycle Cities Nepal

Civil society organisations like Cycle City Network Nepal are advocating for protected cycle lanes, improved pedestrian crossings, and car-free zones in Kathmandu's historic areas. Thamel — the tourist district — has already experimented with pedestrianisation with encouraging results for air quality and street life.

What Students Can Do

You don't need a car to have an impact — or avoid one to make a difference. Your transport choices, voice, and activism all count.
  1. Choose the Safa Tempo or electric bus. Every ride you take on clean public transport instead of a private vehicle or diesel taxi directly reduces Kathmandu's pollution.
  2. Walk or cycle for short journeys. Journeys under 3 km by foot or bicycle produce zero emissions and often take less time than sitting in Kathmandu's traffic.
  3. Advocate on campus. Lobby your university for secure bicycle parking, a cycle loan scheme, and shuttle buses to rail or bus stations.
  4. Join transport advocacy groups. Cycle City Network Nepal and Clean Air Network Nepal welcome student volunteers and campaigners.
  5. Share and normalise sustainable transport. Post about your Safa Tempo rides, your cycling routes, or your walk to campus. Normalising clean transport choices shifts culture.

Cities & Countries Leading the Change

Kathmandu's Electric Future

Nepal's government has set a target of 25% electric vehicles in its national fleet by 2025 and 90% by 2030. With the country's hydropower resources, this is an achievable and genuinely clean target — unlike EV transitions in countries still dependent on fossil-fuel electricity generation.

Pokhara: A Greener City Model

Pokhara, Nepal's second-largest city and tourism hub, has been more successful than Kathmandu in maintaining air quality. Lower vehicle density, lakeside cycling paths, and strict controls on heavy vehicles near Phewa Lake make it a model for what sustainable urban transport in Nepal can look like.

Global Lessons for Nepal

Bogotá's Ciclovía — weekly car-free Sunday streets — has been adopted by dozens of cities and shows how quickly urban culture around transport can shift when given space. Kathmandu's Ason and New Road areas could trial similar pedestrian days. Amsterdam's cycling infrastructure — built over decades through sustained investment and political commitment — demonstrates what Nepal's cities could achieve with the same resolve.
