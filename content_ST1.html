Renewable Energy Article

Nepal's Renewable Energy Opportunity

Nepal is one of the world's richest countries in renewable energy potential. Nestled in the Himalayas, its rivers cascade down steep mountain valleys, offering extraordinary hydropower resources. Yet despite this natural wealth, nearly 15% of Nepal's population still lacks reliable electricity access, and the country remains highly vulnerable to the impacts of climate change it barely contributed to.

SDG 13 calls for urgent action on climate change. For Nepal, transitioning to clean, renewable energy is both a climate imperative and a development opportunity — reducing dependence on imported fossil fuels, cutting carbon emissions, and powering communities that have waited too long for reliable electricity.

Hydropower: Nepal's Blue Gold

Nepal's rivers — fed by Himalayan glaciers and monsoon rains — give the country an estimated theoretical hydropower potential of 83,000 megawatts, making it one of the highest in the world per capita. Hydropower already supplies over 90% of Nepal's installed electricity generation capacity.

Upper Tamakoshi Hydroelectric Project

The Upper Tamakoshi project, with a capacity of 456 MW, is Nepal's largest domestically funded hydroelectric project. Completed in 2021, it dramatically reduced Nepal's dependence on Indian electricity imports and demonstrated that the country can finance and build major infrastructure independently.

The Climate Risk

Climate change poses a paradoxical threat to Nepal's hydropower: glacial retreat will initially increase river flows, but over the long term will reduce them dramatically. Extreme weather events — floods, landslides, and glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs) — also threaten dam infrastructure. Climate-resilient hydropower design and diversification into other renewables are therefore essential.

Key Fact

Nepal has the second-largest water resources in Asia after China, yet exploits less than 4% of its hydropower potential. The climate action imperative and economic opportunity are both vast.

Solar Energy: Power for the Hills

For remote mountain communities that grid infrastructure cannot reach, solar power is often the most practical and affordable solution. Nepal's high altitude and clear skies in many regions make solar irradiation levels excellent — particularly in the Terai plains and mid-hill areas.

Off-Grid Solar Systems

The Alternative Energy Promotion Centre (AEPC), supported by the government and international donors, has facilitated the installation of over 1 million solar home systems across Nepal. These systems provide lighting, phone charging, and basic electricity to households far beyond the reach of the national grid.

Solar Mini-Grids

Community-scale solar mini-grids are providing electricity to entire villages in remote districts including Humla, Dolpa, and Mustang — places where extending the national grid would cost far more per household than a shared solar installation. These mini-grids also support small businesses, schools, and health posts.

Clean energy diversification is crucial for Nepal's climate-resilient future.

Challenges to Overcome

  • Investment gaps: Large hydropower projects require billions in upfront capital. Attracting private investment while protecting public interests is a continuing challenge.
  • Glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs): As Himalayan glaciers melt due to climate change, glacial lakes are growing rapidly. A GLOF can destroy downstream infrastructure including dams and power lines within hours.
  • Earthquake risk: Nepal sits in a highly seismically active zone. All energy infrastructure must be designed to withstand major earthquakes — adding cost and complexity.
  • Transmission losses: Nepal loses approximately 20% of generated electricity through an aging and undersized transmission network. Upgrading the grid is as important as building new generation capacity.
  • Political instability: Frequent changes of government have disrupted long-term energy planning and slowed project approvals.
  • Forest and biodiversity impacts: Large hydropower dams can cause significant habitat loss and affect downstream river ecosystems. Environmental impact assessments must be rigorous and transparent.

What Students Can Do

As a student — whether studying in Kathmandu, Pokhara, or abroad — you have real power to accelerate Nepal's clean energy transition. Here's how:

5 Actions You Can Take Today

1. Switch to LED bulbs and unplug devices not in use — reducing demand matters.
2. Advocate for solar panels on your university campus or hostel rooftop.
3. Study energy policy and share what you learn with your community.
4. Support and amplify the work of AEPC, WWF Nepal, and local energy NGOs.
5. Reduce dependence on diesel generators by lobbying for grid improvement.

Students studying engineering, economics, environmental science, and policy are especially well-placed to contribute directly. Nepal's energy sector needs the next generation of Nepali professionals who understand both the technical and social dimensions of clean energy transitions.

Inspiring Case Studies from Nepal

Kulekhani Reservoir — Pumped Storage Pioneer

The Kulekhani reservoir near Kathmandu serves as Nepal's primary pumped-storage hydroelectric facility. During the 2015 earthquake, it survived intact and continued supplying power to Kathmandu — demonstrating the importance of resilient, distributed energy systems during disasters.

Rural Electrification via Micro-Hydro

Hundreds of small micro-hydro schemes — generating between 5 kW and 100 kW — have brought electricity to remote hill communities across Nepal for the first time. Community-owned and operated, these schemes demonstrate that clean energy and local empowerment go hand in hand. The Practical Action Nepal programme has been instrumental in supporting these community energy projects.

Nepal's Power Trade with India

Nepal now exports surplus hydropower to India during monsoon months when river flows are highest — earning valuable foreign exchange and demonstrating the country's potential as a regional clean energy exporter. The 2023 power trade agreement with India set a landmark precedent for South Asian energy cooperation.

Community involvement is central to Nepal's rural energy access story.

Get Involved

Ready to make a difference? Here are concrete steps to support Nepal's clean energy future:

  • Explore internships with the Alternative Energy Promotion Centre (AEPC).
  • Join student chapters of Engineers Without Borders working on Nepal energy access.
  • Donate to or volunteer with Practical Action Nepal's micro-hydro programmes.
  • Advocate for your university to source renewable electricity.
  • Raise awareness on social media using #SDG13 and #CleanEnergyNepal.
